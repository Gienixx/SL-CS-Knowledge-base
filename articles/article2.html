4 Brilliant tips for Dealing with Angry Customers

Updated 10/14/2024 By Avery Augustine

The Muse offers four ways to calm tense moments. Small things, like using a customer's name, can shift the whole tone.

Angry customers are one of the hardest parts of support work. The Muse offers four ways to calm tense moments. Small things, like using a customer's name, can shift the whole tone.

I’m not exactly a people person. Or, rather, I’m not exactly an angry people person.

But in my job in a technical support department, I have to talk to not-so-satisfied customers fairly often. And in the beginning, my conversations usually sounded a little like this:

“I’m so sorry, but…”
“What we can do is…”
“I know you’re upset and…”

I couldn’t get a word in edgewise. My customers were relentless, I was hesitant, and as a result, I usually wasn’t able to actually solve the problem.

Then, a new employee, Dean, came on board. He sat cattycorner to me, so I constantly overheard his phone conversations and immediately recognized the ease he had when he dealt with angry clients. He never lost his cool, hardly ever had to transfer the call to our boss, and somehow, never let the angry client affect his good mood.

He shared some tips that have helped me learn to listen better, stammer less, and hang up the phone with a sense of accomplishment instead of dread.

1. There’s Power in a Name

One of the first things I noticed about Dean’s conversations was how many times he interjected the caller’s name into the conversation.

Addressing your client with their name sounds a lot more sincere than using a generic label. Once you use a name, you’re speaking with a real person who has a legitimate reason behind the frustration.

2. Smile When You Talk

Smiling when you speak helps your voice convey friendliness and openness. Staying straight-faced removes that warmth from your voice.

For angry clients, you might have to fake it, but forcing a smile does wonders when it comes to calming someone down.

3. Connect With Your Audience

Some customers want to get straight to the point. Others want more pleasantries. The key is to listen to the tone, knowledge, and personality of the person you’re talking to.

This kind of mirroring helps the customer feel understood and more open to your assistance.

4. Let Them Vent

Often, clients are looking to release their frustration. Giving the client full rein over the conversation for the first few minutes helps them blow off some steam and eventually focus on getting to a resolution.

Sometimes the best move is to mute, listen, and let the moment pass before offering a solution.
